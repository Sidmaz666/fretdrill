  2059→                  <p className="text-[11px] text-[#4a4a4a]">The most essential scale in rock, blues, and pop. Contains 5 notes: Root, flat 3rd, 4th, 5th, and flat 7th.   2063→                  <p className="text-[11px] text-[#4a4a4a]">The happy counterpart to the minor pentatonic. Contains Root, 2nd, 3rd, 5th, and 6th.   2067→                  <p className="text-[11px] text-[#4a4a4a]">The minor pentatonic with one crucial addition: the flat 5th (the blue note).   2068→                </div>
  2069→                <div className="border-l-3 border-[#4a5a8a] pl-3">

  2071→                  <p className="text-[11px] text-[#4a4a4a]">The mother of all Western music theory. Seven notes: Root, 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th, 6th, 7th.   2074→                  <h3 className="font-bold text-[12px]">Natural Minor (Aeolian)</h3>
  2075→                  <p className="text-[11px] text-[#4a4a4a]">The sixth mode of the major scale. Darker and more somber than the major scale, it is the foundation of rock and metal.   2079→                  <p className="text-[11px] text-[#4a4a4a]">The natural minor with a raised 7th (natural 7th instead of flat 7th).   2325→                  <h3 className="font-bold text-[12px] mb-1">Intervals</h3>
  2326→                  <p className="text-[11px] mb-1">Intervals are the distance between two notes, measured in semitones (half steps). Every scale, chord, and melody is built from intervals. Understanding them is the key to ear training and improvisation.</p>
  2327→                  <div className="bg-[#f5f0e8] rounded border border-[#c4b89c] p-2 text-[10px]">
  2328→                    <div className="grid grid-cols-2 gap-x-4 gap-y-0.5">
  2329→                      <span><strong>0 semitones</strong> — Unison (same note)</span>
  2330→                      <span><strong>1 semitone</strong> — Minor 2nd</span>
  2331→                      <span><strong>2 semitones</strong> — Major 2nd</span>
  2332→                      <span><strong>3 semitones</strong> — Minor 3rd</span>
  2333→                      <span><strong>4 semitones</strong> — Major 3rd</span>
  2334→                      <span><strong>5 semitones</strong> — Perfect 4th</span>
  2335→                      <span><strong>6 semitones</strong> — Tritone (aug 4th / dim 5th)</span>
  2336→                      <span><strong>7 semitones</strong> — Perfect 5th</span>
  2337→                      <span><strong>8 semitones</strong> — Minor 6th</span>
  2338→                      <span><strong>9 semitones</strong> — Major 6th</span>
  2339→                      <span><strong>10 semitones</strong> — Minor 7th</span>
  2340→                      <span><strong>11 semitones</strong> — Major 7th</span>

  2346→                  <h3 className="font-bold text-[12px] mb-1">The Nashville Number System</h3>
  2347→                  <p className="text-[11px]">FretBoard Forge uses scale degrees (R, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 for major; R, flat 3, 4, 5, flat 7 for minor pentatonic, etc.) to label notes. This is similar to the Nashville Number System used by session musicians worldwide. By thinking in numbers rather than note names, you can transpose any pattern to any key instantly — just change the root and the intervals stay the same.</p>
  2348→                </div>
  2349→                <div>
  2350→                  <h3 className="font-bold text-[12px] mb-1">Relative Major/Minor</h3>
  2351→                  <p className="text-[11px]">Every major key has a relative minor key that shares the same notes (and vice versa). The relative minor is found 3 semitones below the major root. For example, C Major and A Minor share identical notes — the difference is which note feels like home. This is why the minor pentatonic and major pentatonic shapes overlap on the fretboard.</p>
  2352→                </div>
  2353→                <div>
  2354→                  <h3 className="font-bold text-[12px] mb-1">Chord Construction</h3>
  2355→                  <p className="text-[11px]">Chords are built by stacking intervals: Major triad = Root + Major 3rd + Perfect 5th. Minor triad = Root + Minor 3rd + Perfect 5th. Diminished = Root + Minor 3rd + Diminished 5th. Augmented = Root + Major 3rd + Augmented 5th. Seventh chords add a fourth note. Understanding construction lets you find any chord anywhere on the neck.</p>
  2356→                </div>
  2357→                <div>
  2358→                  <h3 className="font-bold text-[12px] mb-1">Diatonic Harmony</h3>
  2359→                  <p className="text-[11px]">In any major key, chords naturally occur in this order: I (major), ii (minor), iii (minor), IV (major), V (major), vi (minor), viio (diminished). This is the foundation of most Western music. The V7 chord resolving to I is the strongest cadence in music. Practice the Chord Scale exercise to see and hear this relationship on the fretboard.</p>
  2360→                </div>
  2361→                <div>
  2362→                  <h3 className="font-bold text-[12px] mb-1">The Fretboard Layout</h3>
  2363→                  <p className="text-[11px]">The guitar fretboard repeats every 12 frets (one octave). Fret 12 is the octave of the open string. The dots at frets 3, 5, 7, 9, and the double dot at 12 are visual landmarks. Learning the notes on the low E and A strings first gives you anchor points for finding every other note using octave shapes. The Note Finder and Octave Drill exercises are specifically designed to build this knowledge.</p>
  2364→                </div>
  2365→                <div>
  2366→                  <h3 className="font-bold text-[12px] mb-1">String Tuning and Standard Tuning</h3>
  2367→                  <p className="text-[11px]">FretBoard Forge uses standard tuning: E-A-D-G-B-E (low to high). The intervals between adjacent strings are: 4th, 4th, 4th, Major 3rd, 4th — that Major 3rd between G and B strings is the B string bump that makes guitar tuning unique and requires a fret adjustment when crossing between the G and B strings.</p>
